Intake Service Desk with Auto-Routing

Marketing campaign requests were tracked in a tool with no structure, no reporting, and no shared process. I designed and built a JSM intake system with a large conditional form, three automations, and five fully custom team projects. A single submitted request automatically generates and routes tasks to every team involved.

Details in this project have been generalized to protect confidential information. The system described is based on work I designed and built in a production environment.

The Problem

Campaign requests were managed in a work tool that had no real structure. Each team knew their piece of the process. Nobody had the full picture. There was no reporting, no accountability, and no visibility for management unless they lived inside the tool every day. Searching was essentially impossible. The process existed mostly as tribal knowledge.

Requests could involve multiple teams depending on what the campaign needed — design, video, digital ads, social media — and there was no reliable way to make sure every team got what they needed to start their work. Things fell through the gaps regularly.

How the Routing Works

A request comes in through a JSM portal. The intake form uses conditional logic to reveal team-specific sections based on what the submitter selects. Once submitted, a reviewer checks the ticket before it moves forward. Changing the status to "Tasks created" triggers three automations simultaneously. Each automation uses branched field-value logic to generate the right tasks in the right team projects. All generated tasks link back to the original JSM ticket using custom link labels.

What I Built
01
Requirements discovery
No documented process existed. I ran independent sessions with each team over about a month, whiteboarding and flowcharting what each team actually needed from a request. The process was mostly tribal knowledge — I had to pull it out one question at a time and push back when answers seemed incomplete. It took multiple sessions per team because one team's answer would reveal something another team hadn't mentioned yet.
02
Conditional intake form
Built using Proforma (now Atlassian Forms natively in JSM). The form is large — team selection at the top controls which sections appear below. Each team section has its own questions, and those questions have further conditional branches based on what's selected. Digital Ads, for example, presents every available ad type and each selection reveals additional spec fields. The form captures everything needed to generate tasks without follow-up.
03
Human review gate
Submitted tickets enter a pending review status before any automation fires. A reviewer checks that the ticket is complete and accurate. Only when the status is manually changed to "Tasks created" do the automations trigger. This step was added to catch bad submissions before they generated a cascade of tasks that would all need to be corrected or deleted.
04
Three routing automations
Split across three automations due to Atlassian's per-rule item limits. Automation 1 handles Design teams (Digital Design and Web Design). Automation 2 handles Digital Ads. Automation 3 handles Video and Social Media. Each uses branched field-value logic — if a field has a specific value, generate a task; if not, move to the next branch. Digital Ads generates one separate task per ad type selected, so a single request could produce several tasks for that team alone.
05
Five custom team projects
Built dedicated Jira Software projects for Digital Design, Web Design, Digital Ads, Video, and Social Media. Each has custom issue types, custom workflows, custom fields, and custom screens — nothing out of the box. The JSM project is equally custom. Teams manage their own work in their project and communicate on the original JSM ticket.
06
Custom link structure
Standard Jira issue linking with custom label names. The JSM ticket shows linked tasks under "Marketing children." Each team task shows the JSM ticket under "Marketing parent." This gave the visual hierarchy of a parent/child relationship without using Jira's native work item hierarchy, and let the close automation target only the linked children when checking completion status.
07
Confluence knowledge base
A Confluence space tied to the JSM project gave submitters reference material while filling out the form. Help text in the form linked directly to relevant Confluence pages. Each team also had restricted Confluence pages for internal process documentation and communication.

The Outcome

What had been an unstructured, manually coordinated process running across multiple teams with no visibility became a single submission that handled all the routing automatically. Management could see every active request, every linked task, and where things stood across all teams at any point.

The system ran from go-live through my departure and is still in use. It handled roughly 2,500 requests per month at scale. It was expanded over time to include additional request types, and Vendor Relations and larger group campaign workflows were added after the initial launch.

Most issues after go-live were resolved within a day. Eventually I could turn around form and automation fixes in under 30 minutes. The initial build had a compressed 3-month timeline — the system evolved in the months after launch as edge cases surfaced and the process became better understood.
