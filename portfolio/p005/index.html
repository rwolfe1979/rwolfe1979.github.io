Marketplace App Evaluation Framework

There was no process for evaluating Marketplace apps before I owned it. Requests went directly to a budget conversation with no technical review, no security check, and no assessment of whether the app overlapped with something already installed. I built a repeatable evaluation framework covering security, cost, cross-team benefit, and vendor risk. It gave leadership actual information to make decisions.

Details in this project have been generalized to protect confidential information. The system described is based on work I designed and managed in a production Atlassian Cloud environment.

The Problem

Before I took over platform administration, app requests had no standard path. A department leader would approach the IT director directly, make the case for a new tool, and if the budget conversation went well, the app got installed. Nobody checked whether the app overlapped with something already in the instance. Nobody reviewed the vendor's security profile. Nobody asked whether more than one team could benefit from the same license.

The result was a growing catalog of apps that no team fully understood. Some were used by one person. Some weren't being used at all. And removing any of them carried risk. You can't always know what configuration an app has touched until after it's gone.

The cleanest solution was to stop the problem at the door. That meant having a consistent process that every request went through before any decision got made.

What I Built
01
JSM-based request intake
All app evaluation requests came in through the Jira Service Management portal. Standardized intake meant every request included the requester's name, the app they wanted, and their stated use cases. If someone couldn't explain why they needed an app, that was worth knowing before any evaluation work started.
02
Tiered evaluation process
Paid and free apps followed different review tracks. Free apps got a lighter security and compatibility check. Paid apps went through a multi-step process covering use cases, alternatives, security, compatibility, and cost before any leadership conversation happened. The tier determined how much process the request needed, not who was asking.
03
Marketplace security review
Every paid app request included a review of the vendor's Atlassian Marketplace security profile. I checked four things: whether the vendor had completed Atlassian's privacy questionnaire, whether the app was part of the Marketplace Bug Bounty program, whether the vendor had a Trust Center, and what integration permissions the app required. An app that needed more access than it could justify was a flag.
04
30-day trial management
Where available, I ran a 30-day trial before committing to a contract. This was especially useful for more complex apps where the full feature set wasn't clear from the listing alone. Trials let me see actual performance before any purchase decision was made. The requesting team also got a chance to confirm the app did what they thought it would.
05
Partner contract coordination
The instance ran on a managed Atlassian partner contract, not direct Atlassian billing. That structure offered meaningful cost savings but meant approved paid apps required a contract amendment. I coordinated those updates directly with the partner, verifying user counts, product associations, and prorated billing, before any app went live.
06
App registry
I documented every installed app with its product association, category, description, and links to the vendor's Marketplace listing and help documentation. The registry served as the internal reference for anyone asking what a given app did, which product it ran in, and whether it was still active. It also supported the yearly audit process.
07
Yearly audit
At each contract renewal I reviewed every active app for usage and cost justification. The questions were simple: Is this still being used? Does the cost still make sense? Has anything changed about the team using it? Apps that didn't pass that review were removed. I removed about five apps over two audit cycles. Free apps were also reviewed for any pricing changes during this period.

The Outcome

The framework kept the instance clean and the costs defensible. At each contract renewal, I could account for every app in the catalog. What it did, who was using it, and whether it was still worth the cost. The yearly audit removed five apps over two cycles without any of the risk that comes from removing something that's been in use for a while.

More importantly, the framework prevented the harder problem. Removing an app after it's embedded in someone's workflow is always riskier than not adding it in the first place. You can't always predict what configuration an app has touched until after it's gone. It's better to prevent than to clean up.

Leadership got clear recommendations backed by research instead of vendor marketing. The IT director didn't have to make decisions on incomplete information. And the instance didn't accumulate tools that nobody asked for twice.
