Blocker Priority Dashboard

Back-end teams were losing time to blocker tickets submitted without a real reason. I designed a custom conditional field structure across three service desks to capture the reason behind every blocker, built a Jira dashboard to surface the data to leadership, and tied Paid Rush requests to a monthly billing process.

Details in this project have been generalized to protect confidential information. The system described is based on work I designed and built in a production environment.

The Problem

Blocker priority meant everything stopped. If a ticket came in marked Blocker, back-end teams dropped what they were doing and handled it immediately. That's what the priority was for.

The problem was that people were using it freely. No reason required, no approval needed, no visibility into whether a given ticket actually warranted interrupting everything else. Back-end teams were raising it as a recurring complaint. Leadership had no data to respond with. Without structured data on why blockers were being submitted, there was no way to distinguish legitimate use from misuse.

Field Logic Flow

When Blocker priority is selected, a Blocker Reason field appears. The selection routes to a different downstream field depending on the reason. Error reveals a sub-type selector. Supervisor Approved reveals a scoped user picker. Paid Rush shows nothing and goes straight to submission.

What I Built
01
Three custom fields across three service desks
Blocker Reason (select list: Error, Supervisor Approved, Paid Rush), Error Sub-type (select list with predefined categories and Other), and Approving Supervisor (user picker scoped to the supervisor permission group). All three were added to the Marketing, Catalog, and Development service desks.
02
Conditional form logic
Built in Atlassian Forms (formerly Proforma). The Blocker Reason field only appears when Blocker priority is selected. Choosing Error reveals the Error Sub-type field. Choosing Supervisor Approved reveals the Approving Supervisor user picker. Paid Rush showed nothing additional and went straight to submission.
03
Warning messaging
Added warning text to the form when Blocker was selected. It stated clearly that abuse of the priority would result in oversight. This wasn't enforcement on its own — it was visibility. A lot of the reduction in blocker volume came from customer service reps realizing that someone was watching the data.
04
Jira dashboard
Built with pie charts and 2D filter gadgets. It showed the distribution of blocker reason types, error sub-types, and supervisor usage at a glance. All data was pulled from JQL saved filters. Any gadget could be clicked to drill into the underlying ticket list. Leadership got a summary view without needing to write their own JQL.
05
Paid Rush billing integration
Paid Rush tickets are pulled monthly from a JQL filter and used to generate client invoices. The dashboard data feeds into the billing process. The VP came to me with the paid rush concept. I designed the field structure and data capture that made billing possible — the original ask was just "charge for it." I built the system that makes that actually work.

The Outcome

Blocker volume dropped after the system went live. The form made submitting a blocker harder to do without a stated reason. The warning message added visibility. The supervisor approval path created a paper trail. I don't have an exact count on the reduction, but the back-end teams stopped raising it as a recurring complaint.

The dashboard is still active. The Paid Rush billing pull runs every month. The data exists now in a form that leadership can act on, which is what they asked for.
