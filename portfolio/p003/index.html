Blocker Priority Dashboard

Back-end teams were losing time to blocker tickets submitted without a real reason. I designed a custom conditional field structure across three service desks to capture the reason behind every blocker, built a Jira dashboard to surface the data to leadership, and tied Paid Rush requests to a monthly billing process.

Details in this project have been generalized to protect confidential information. The system described is based on work I designed and built in a production environment.

The Problem

Blocker priority meant everything stopped. If a ticket came in marked Blocker, back-end teams dropped what they were doing and handled it immediately. That's what the priority was for. The problem was that anyone submitting a request could select Blocker with no requirement to justify it. Nobody was tracking how often it happened or why.

Back-end teams raised it as a recurring complaint. Volume was high and the reasons were invisible. Leadership wanted to stop the abuse and get data on what was actually driving blocker requests. They also wanted to introduce a paid option for clients who needed something expedited. The field system I designed covered all of it.

How It Works

When a customer service rep sets a ticket to Blocker priority, a Blocker Reason field appears on the form. Selecting a reason triggers further conditional fields: an error sub-type selector or a supervisor user picker, depending on the selection. Paid Rush requires no extra fields and routes to billing. All three paths log data that the dashboard reads from JQL saved filters.

What I Built
01
Custom Blocker Reason field
A select field with three options: Error, Supervisor Approved, and Paid Rush. Added to all three service desks: Marketing, Catalog, and Development. It only appears on the form when Blocker priority is selected. Before this, priority selection had no accountability attached to it. Anyone could mark a ticket Blocker and submit.
02
Conditional form logic
Built with Atlassian Forms (formerly Proforma). Selecting Error reveals a second field for the specific error type, with a list of common categories and an Other option. Selecting Supervisor Approved reveals a user picker scoped to a permission group that contained only supervisors. No free-text, no ambiguity about who approved it. Paid Rush showed nothing additional and went straight to submission.
03
Warning messaging
Added warning text to the form when Blocker was selected. It stated clearly that abuse of the priority would result in oversight. This wasn't enforcement on its own. It was visibility. A lot of the reduction in blocker volume came from customer service reps realizing that someone was watching the data.
04
Jira dashboard
Built with pie charts and 2D filter gadgets. It showed the distribution of blocker reason types, error sub-types, and supervisor usage at a glance. All data was pulled from JQL saved filters. Any gadget could be clicked to drill into the underlying ticket list. Leadership got a summary view without needing to write their own JQL.
05
Paid Rush billing integration
Paid Rush tickets are pulled monthly from a JQL filter and used to generate client invoices. The dashboard data feeds into the billing process. The VP came to me with the paid rush concept. I designed the field structure and data capture that made billing possible. The original ask was just "charge for it." I built the system that makes that actually work.

The Outcome

Blocker volume dropped after the system went live. The form made submitting a blocker harder to do without a stated reason. The warning message added visibility. The supervisor approval path created a paper trail. I don't have an exact count on the reduction, but the back-end teams stopped complaining about it.

The dashboard is still active. The Paid Rush billing process runs every month. That part of the system became a permanent part of how the organization invoices clients for expedited work.
